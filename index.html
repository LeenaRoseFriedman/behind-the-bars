365 Days Outside

If someone stays out of prison for one year, the system loses ~$100,000.

Is the system failing — or working exactly as designed?

You are 12 years old.

Your parents struggle with addiction.

No one is guiding you.

Your environment teaches survival.

The system calls it criminal.

This is Not Random

It is not individual decisions that cause people to end up in prison.

People’s lives are shaped and targeted long before being arrested, by systemic inequalities, discrimination, instability, addiction in the home, and environments where survival is the main thing on one’s mind.

After those conditions are solidified, the system steps in to intervene.

Not to uplift those in need, provide rehabilitation or resources, but to punish the outcomes.

The System Works Exactly As Designed

The United States has the highest incarceration rate in the world.

The system was built on the idea of rehabilitation — but in reality, it functions as punishment, control, and removal.

People are sent to prison for survival behaviors, untreated mental health issues, addiction, and poverty.

“Nobody’s being corrected. It’s just the name.”

“Prison shouldn’t be the first line of defense. It should be the last.”

The Scale

Over 2 million people are incarcerated in the United States.

77% are rearrested within 5 years.

If this system was working as rehabilitation, these numbers would not exist.

This is a cycle, not a solution.

Why The Prison System Fails

1. It punishes instead of treating
Mental illness, trauma, and addiction are criminalized instead of addressed.

2. It creates more harm
Solitary confinement, violence, and isolation increase long-term instability rather than rehabilitation.

3. It blocks re-entry
People leave with records that prevent jobs, housing, and education.

4. It targets specific communities
Over-policing and systemic inequality funnel certain populations into prison.

Where People Return

Reentry is not equal. Some communities are forced to carry the weight of this cycle more than others.

A Voice From Inside

Edgardo Patron — Formerly incarcerated

“Each time I help someone stay out 365 days — that’s $100,000 the system doesn’t see. And if I teach that person to teach 10 other people, that’s 1 million dollars that they don’t see, and so on and so forth. And when they say, who’s hand is in my pocket? I’m gonna say, ‘Hey, the twelve year old boy you put in solitary confinement.’”

The system clearly isn’t meant for rehabilitation. Putting a 12-year-old boy, who grew up in a bad situation without the same opportunities as others, into solitary confinement isn’t protecting society — it’s targeting people who are already vulnerable.

Another Perspective

Reintegration Is Public Safety

When people leave prison, they are expected to rebuild their lives immediately.

But how, when people who have been part of the prison system are treated as less than a person?

  • No stable housing
  • Limited employment opportunities
  • Stigma and surveillance
  • Little to no mental health support

Without support, the likelihood of returning to prison increases.

Reintegration is not optional — it determines whether cycles continue.

HangTime

HangTime is a program made to bring together ex-offenders, community leaders, and legal professionals into one family, united by similar adversities, and focuses on empowering those involved.

The program is meant for those recovering from similar experiences, but anyone is welcome.

In my time there, I have never felt any less a part of the community than anyone there.

While being there, we learned about healthy habits for one's body, and it was so special sitting there learning all together, normalizing the importance of education for those who have never had the opportunity to learn in a safe and effective environment.

“Our programs are designed to provide support and guidance to the youth in our communities. Through mentorship and community engagement, we aim to create a positive impact and foster personal growth.”

The Question

When people return to society post-incarceration, how do we break the barriers and obstacles the system has built up?

What happens after prison determines everything.
